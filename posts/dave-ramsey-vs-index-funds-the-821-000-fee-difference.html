The Hook

A 1% fee — one percent — will cost you $821,000 over your investing lifetime. Not a typo. Eight hundred twenty-one thousand dollars. And right now, one of the most famous names in personal finance is telling millions of people to pay it. Let me show you the actual math, because nobody else will.

Background

So this week, Caleb Hammer versus Dave Ramsey is all over the finance internet — U.S. News even ran a full comparison of their investing advice. Everyone's arguing about the drama. Nobody's arguing about the math. So let's do that. Picture Maya. She's 27, makes $62,000 a year as a project coordinator, and she just paid off her last credit card using Ramsey's baby steps — which, credit where it's due, genuinely work for getting out of debt. Now she's got $500 a month freed up and she's ready to invest. She follows the Ramsey playbook: find 'good growth stock mutual funds' through an advisor. The funds she's shown have solid track records — and expense ratios around 1%, sometimes with a sales load on top. One percent. Maya shrugs. What's one percent? A rounding error, right? That shrug — that exact moment — is the single most expensive decision Maya will make in her entire life. More expensive than any car. More expensive than most houses. And she'll never see it happen, because it never shows up as a line item anywhere.
