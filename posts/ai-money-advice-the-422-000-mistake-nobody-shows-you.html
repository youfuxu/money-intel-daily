The Hook

$422,000. That's the difference between following one confidently wrong answer from an AI chatbot and running the actual math yourself. Same starting money. Same 30 years. Same person. And according to a study that just hit CNBC this week, millions of people are on the wrong side of that number right now — and they don't even know it.

Background

Let me introduce you to Maya. She's 27, makes $61,000 a year, and just crossed $5,000 in savings for the first time in her life. Huge moment. So she does what most of us would do in 2026 — she opens ChatGPT and types: 'I have $5,000 saved. What should I do with it?' The answer sounds smart. Polished. Confident. It tells her to keep her money 'safe and liquid' in a savings account, mentions an interest rate that hasn't been accurate in two years, and — here's the killer — never once asks if her employer offers a 401(k) match. Hers does. Fifty cents on the dollar, up to 6%. That's a guaranteed 50% return she's leaving on the table, and the AI never brought it up because it doesn't know she exists. Maya follows the advice. Feels responsible. Feels done. And that one conversation — five minutes, zero pushback — quietly sets her on a path that costs her more than the price of a house. The new study found AI chatbots gave outdated, incomplete, or incorrect personal finance guidance in a huge share of test cases. Not because they're dumb.
