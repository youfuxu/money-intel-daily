The Hook

Six hundred and six thousand dollars. That's the difference between investing $150 a month in a boring index fund versus investing the exact same $150 a month the way most Gen Z investors are actually doing it right now — picking hot stocks and trading in and out. Same money. Same 40 years. One path ends at $948,000. The other ends at $342,000. The gap isn't luck. It's math, and today I'm going to show you exactly where that $606,000 disappears.

Background

Meet Maya. She's 24, makes $58,000 a year working in marketing, and honestly? She's ahead of most people her age. She's got $6,000 in a Robinhood account and she actually invests every month — which puts her in rare company. Here's the problem. Maya's portfolio is seven individual stocks she found on TikTok and Reddit, plus a little crypto. Last year she was up 40% for about three weeks and screenshotted it to her group chat. Then two of her picks dropped 60%, she panic-sold at the bottom, sat in cash for four months waiting for 'the right time to get back in,' and missed the rebound entirely. Sound familiar? This is exactly the pattern The Times reported on this week: Gen Z is powering the biggest retail investing boom in history — more of us are investing, earlier, than any generation ever — but the data suggests a huge chunk of us are doing it in the one way that's statistically proven to lose. Maya isn't failing because she's dumb. She's failing because she's playing the wrong game. And the cost of playing the wrong game, over a career, is life-changing money.
